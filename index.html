Georgia's Perspective

What types of rules or agreements exist to ensure a fair distribution of water from the Black Sea between countries?

The focus of the rules and agreements in Georgia are on maritime use, environmental protection and shipping rights. There are several regulations, such as Bucharest Convention(1992), United Nation Convention on the Law of the Sea and Black Sea Economic Cooperation.

  • Bucharest Convention(1992)-The Black Sea Commission is the regional governing body created to implement the Bucharest Convention signed by the six coastal countries in 1992. The areas of work of the Black Sea Commission are, inter alia, to monitor and assess pollution, control pollution from land-based sources, ensure conservation of biological diversity, address environmental safety aspects of shipping, address environmental aspects of management of fisheries and other marine living resources(UN Environment programme, n.d.).
  • United Nation Convention on the Law of the Sea -The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea was adopted in 1982. It lays down a comprehensive regime of law and order in the world's oceans and seas establishing rules governing all uses of the oceans and their resources. It embodies in one instrument traditional rules for the uses of the oceans and at the same time introduces new legal concepts(IMO, n.d.)
  • Black Sea Economic Cooperation is a unique and promising model of multilateral political and economic initiative with the signing of the Istanbul Summit Declaration and the Bosphorus Statement by the Heads of State and Government of the countries in the region, on 25 June 1992. BSEC covers a geography encompassing the territories of the Black Sea littoral States, the Balkans and the Caucasus with an area of nearly 20 million square kilometers. The BSEC region is located on two continents(BSEC, n.d.)

Turkey's Perspective

What types of rules or agreements exist to ensure a fair distribution of water from the Black Sea between countries?

Turkey is involved in both bilateral and regional agreements that regulate water sharing in the Black Sea region.

  • The most important is the 1927 Treaty on the Beneficial Uses of Boundary Waters, also known as the Kars Protocol. It was signed with the USSR and renewed with Georgia in 1990. This treaty applies to the Çoruh River, which flows from Turkey into Georgia. It requires equal (50-50) sharing of water, joint monitoring stations, and cooperation in planning any dams or other infrastructure that could affect water flow (Follow the River, 2021). The treaty also includes a compensation mechanism in case of harm, even if precautions are taken.
  • Turkey also participates in regional frameworks such as the Black Sea Strategic Action Plan (BS SAP). This plan was developed with all six Black Sea countries and promotes responsible environmental management. It follows principles like Integrated River Basin Management, which encourages countries to manage water as part of a larger ecosystem rather than focusing on national borders (JRC, 2016). While this plan does not assign water quantities, it supports monitoring, pollution control, and cooperation on freshwater issues across the region. t

How does the system of water sharing between Turkey, Russia, and Georgia in the Black Sea impact sustainable development in the region?

Water sharing between Turkey, Russia, and Georgia affects sustainable development in many ways.

  • For example, Turkey has built hydropower plants on the Çoruh River without consistently working with Georgia. These projects have caused environmental concerns, such as damage to ecosystems and erosion near the river's mouth. Courts and local experts have warned that building dams without protecting the natural balance of the river could lead to irreversible harm (Follow the River, 2021).
  • Although there are agreements in place, their implementation is uneven. The Black Sea Strategic Action Plan tries to promote sustainable development through shared environmental efforts. It includes goals like pollution monitoring and protecting biodiversity (JRC, 2016). However, the region still faces challenges like weak laws, poor coordination, and lack of technical capacity, especially in less developed countries (CGScholar, 2023).
  • Other large projects, such as the Turkish Stream pipeline, show how development goals sometimes take priority over environmental planning. These overlapping interests highlight the need for stronger cooperation and more balanced decision-making (TASS, 2017). n

Are current water-sharing agreements in the Black Sea fair, considering the different levels of development in these countries?

Agreements like the Kars Protocol appear fair on paper.

  • They promise equal sharing and joint responsibility (Follow the River, 2021). However, in reality, fairness depends on how agreements are implemented. Turkey has more technical and financial power than Georgia. It has advanced large dam projects on the Çoruh River without always consulting its neighbor. This puts Georgia at a disadvantage and shows how formal equality may not lead to fair results.
  • Weaker countries often lack the tools to monitor environmental damage or defend their interests. The lack of strong regional laws and limited cooperation systems makes it harder for less developed countries to enforce agreements (CGScholar, 2023). Better monitoring and data access also give stronger countries more power in negotiations. Long-term infrastructure deals, such as the Turkish Stream pipeline, may also shape regional development in ways that benefit wealthier states for decades (TASS, 2017). To achieve real fairness, countries need better enforcement, stronger support for weaker partners, and more open planning across borders.

Conclusion

Key Findings

Water-sharing and maritime agreements in the Black Sea—like the Bucharest Convention, UNCLOS, and BSEC—aim to support fair use, environmental protection, and safe trade. On paper, they treat all countries equally. However, in practice, power differences affect how fair and effective these agreements are.

  • Georgia, with limited resources, depends on cooperation to protect its environment and access trade routes. While agreements help, Georgia often lacks the power to fully enforce its rights, especially when stronger neighbors like Turkey make decisions—such as building dams—without full coordination.
  • Turkey plays a leading role, using its stronger economy and infrastructure to push development, sometimes at the cost of shared environmental planning.
  • Russia uses its military and energy influence to control key routes and shape regional politics, which can limit cooperation and fairness.

Overall, while agreements in the Black Sea region help support peace, trade, and environmental protection, they are not always fair in practice. Stronger countries have more power and resources, which gives them an advantage. To make the system truly fair, rules need to be enforced more equally. Weaker countries like Georgia should get more support so they can fully take part and protect their interests. Better cooperation is also needed, with all countries working together, sharing decisions, and respecting each other’s needs. This is the only way to reach real fairness and long-term sustainability in the region.
